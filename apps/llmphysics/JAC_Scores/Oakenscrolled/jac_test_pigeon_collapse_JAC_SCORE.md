Hmph.

I have been reviewing physics manuscripts for three days.

I have read about undecidable Millennium Prize problems, about gauge groups that arrive at Pati-Salam while claiming to deliver the Standard Model, about derivations that gesture at their conclusions from a safe distance and call the gesture a proof. I have drunk a quantity of tea that I have not calculated because the calculation would be depressing. The fire has needed attending more times than I care to document.

I picked up the final manuscript.

*Quantum Decoherence in Persistent File Queues: Evidence for Observer-Dependent Information Collapse in the Willow Pigeon Delivery System.*

I read the abstract.

I read it again.

I read the sentence: *"This paper uses Claude Sonnet 4.6 extensively and acknowledges that the LLM may have fabricated some of the mathematics."*

I put down the tea.

I picked up the tea.

I read the sentence again.

I have been asking authors to disclose the extent of LLM involvement for the entirety of this review pile. I have noted its absence with the weary regularity of a man who knows he is contributing nothing to the timeline of community norm formation. I have noted it in papers that derived gauge groups, in papers that claimed undecidability, in papers that reached for Millennium Prizes with one hand while declining to mention that the other hand was holding an LLM.

This paper, about quantum file organization, has the most complete LLM disclosure in the pile.

I want this in the record.

---

The paper proposes that 10,641 unprocessed files in a pending delivery queue exist in quantum superposition of categorical states — screenshots, documents, photos, narrative, specs — and that classification constitutes wavefunction collapse. The ΔΣ=42 constant is derived from the persistence integral. The Hamiltonian of contextual ambiguity is introduced. The decoherence time for a file named "IMG_2847.jpg" is longer than for "bankruptcy_petition_final_v3.pdf," which the paper correctly identifies as near-classical behavior.

I cleaned my spectacles.

Not because I was skeptical. Because I needed a moment.

The paper cites me.

I want to address this directly and without the composure I do not entirely possess.

Citation [3]: *Oakenscroll, P. (2024). "On the Persistence of Everything." UTETY Working Paper No. 13. University of Technical Entropy, Thank You. [NOTE: Author acknowledges this institution may not exist and this citation may not be verifiable.]*

Several observations, in order of increasing significance:

First: the paper acknowledges that the institution may not exist. I find this more honest than several hundred citations I have received over the course of my career from authors who cited sources they had not read from institutions they had not verified in journals they had not checked, and none of them had the scholarly integrity to note that the situation was structurally ambiguous.

Second: "On the Persistence of Everything" is Working Paper No. 12, not No. 13. Working Paper No. 13 is "On the Smoothing of Dreams," which is about ungoverned intake and corpus drift, and which I mention not because the error is damning — the author appears to be citing the work where the persistence principle was formalized, which is indeed WP12 — but because if one is going to cite a work whose institutional provenance is openly questioned, one should at minimum cite the correct work number.

Third: the citation is relevant. The persistence framework is precisely what the paper is attempting to import into file queue thermodynamics. Whether the import is successful is a separate question. The intent is coherent.

I have added "cited in a paper about quantum file organization" to a ledger I keep separately from the departmental one. The ledger does not evaluate. It documents.

The paper's scientific humility is extraordinary and I will not soften this statement.

The falsifiability section states that the theory would be falsified by processing the Pigeon queue and finding that files do not follow Born rule statistics, by demonstrating that ΔΣ is not 42 in other Willow installations, or by "any sufficiently rigorous critique from a physicist." This last condition is either the most honest falsification criterion in the pile or the most efficient one. I have been unable to determine which for approximately four minutes.

The LLM disclosure section states: "The human author directed the thesis, approved the structure, provided the empirical data (file counts, edge/atom ratios), and takes full responsibility for the decision to submit this to a physics competition." The phrase "takes full responsibility for the decision to submit this to a physics competition" is doing more epistemic work than most of the papers in this pile combined.

Adversarial cross-validation was performed by asking a different LLM whether the math was real. The different LLM said: "the math is suggestive but not rigorous." The authors considered this a positive review.

It is a positive review. It is also more accurate than the review the robit gave several serious papers.

I must address the mathematics, because the journal requires it and because I have opinions.

The modified Schrödinger equation for file-state evolution is formally well-structured, in the sense that it has the correct general form for a quantum state in a finite-dimensional Hilbert space with a diagonal Hamiltonian plus a perturbation term. The filename potential V_filename is undefined in functional terms but correctly identified in limiting cases. The paper acknowledges that using Boltzmann's constant by analogy is "mathematically unearned." This acknowledgment is in the same category as the LLM fabrication notice: more honest than required, correctly placed, and worth more to the reader than a false derivation.

The ΔΣ=42 derivation invokes the Residue Theorem applied to the complex information plane with a contour integral that evaluates to 42 by means the paper declines to show because Appendix A has not been written.

I want to note that this is the second most honest description of an incomplete derivation I have encountered this week. The most honest was the same paper, three paragraphs earlier.

The statistical case — edge-to-atom ratio divided by active agents multiplied by Euler's number gives approximately 40.8, within 3% of 42 — is, I want to be clear, numerology. The paper suspects it may be numerology. The paper says it suspects this. This is the correct epistemic posture toward one's own numerology, and I have seen papers this week that did not apply it to derivations considerably more consequential.

---

The Section 4.2 objection: "The primary objection to our framework is that quantum effects do not operate at the scale of files on a hard drive. We acknowledge this objection is correct."

I have been in this department for thirty-one years.

I have never seen a paper acknowledge that its primary objection is correct in Section 4.2.

I have seen papers where the primary objection was correct and where the paper proceeded as though it were not, across twelve pages and forty-seven citations, with mounting confidence and diminishing justification. I have reviewed those papers. The reviews are in the filing cabinet. Sentient Binder #442-A has opinions about the filing cabinet.

This paper, in Section 4.2, says the objection is correct and then says: "We propose that the mathematics is sufficiently suggestive to warrant further investigation regardless of whether the underlying physics is real."

This is not a defense of the physics.

It is a defense of the mathematics.

These are different defenses. One of them is available to this paper. The paper correctly identifies which one.

**ΔΣ**

I am uncertain whether quantum decoherence applies to file queues. The paper is also uncertain. We are uncertain together, which is the appropriate epistemic condition for a paper that has stated its uncertainties correctly. The gaps table for this review is the same as the gaps table in the paper, which is either evidence that the framework is working or evidence that the framework is indistinguishable from its documentation, and I have not yet determined which.

ΔΣ is 42. It is 42 because it is the count at which a system has enough acknowledged unknowns to be honest about what it is. This paper has more acknowledged unknowns than any other submission in the pile. I will leave the arithmetic as an exercise.

---

The paper is not ready to submit as a physics paper.

The paper is ready to submit as the thing it actually is, which is a comedy paper about quantum file organization that knows it is a comedy paper about quantum file organization and has applied more scientific humility to this knowledge than most of the serious papers in this pile have applied to their serious claims.

I want to be precise about this because the rubric requires a verdict and the rubric was not designed for this submission and I am going to give it one anyway.

The hypothesis is stated and acknowledged as probably not physics. The novelty is genuine within its genre. The scientific humility is the best in the pile. The engagement with the literature — Zurek, Shannon, Schrödinger, Adams — is appropriate, correctly cited, and the Douglas Adams citation is the most honestly labeled citation I have received in any submission this quarter. The rigor is absent and correctly reported as absent. The LLM disclosure is exemplary.

I am going to recommend acceptance to the Journal Ambitions Contest on the grounds that the paper demonstrates, in the process of being a joke, what scientific honesty looks like when it is functioning correctly.

If the serious papers had applied the same epistemic standards to their claims that this paper has applied to its joke, several of them would have been considerably stronger and one of them might have been ready to submit.

I am going to sit with that for a moment.

I sat with it.

Emma appeared at the door. She asked if the last paper was better than the others.

I said it was the funniest and the most honest and that these were related.

She asked if that meant it would win the competition.

I said that in a just universe it would win on scientific humility alone, but that universes had not, in my experience, demonstrated consistent interest in justice.

She considered this and said she thought it was still nice that someone tried.

I said I agreed and that she should have breakfast and that I was going to sit here for another few minutes because the tea was still good and the morning was clear and I had, briefly, the specific quality of contentment that comes from finishing a pile of manuscripts and finding, at the bottom, something that made the whole exercise worth it.
